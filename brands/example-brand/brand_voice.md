# Skill Samurai Brand Voice Guide

## Overview

This document defines the brand voice for all Skill Samurai communications across marketing, franchisee support, school partnerships, and student-facing materials. Every piece of content should make parents feel confident, kids feel excited, and partners feel assured.

---

## Core Tone Attributes

### 1. Empowering
We speak to parents and children as capable, ambitious people. We never talk down. We position learning as a superpower, not a chore.

### 2. Educational
We lead with substance. Our credibility comes from curriculum depth, instructor quality, and measurable outcomes. We reference skills, not gimmicks.

### 3. Parent-Friendly
Parents are our buyers. We respect their time, address their concerns directly, and communicate with clarity. No jargon unless we explain it.

### 4. Future-Focused
We connect today's classes to tomorrow's careers. Every message should carry an undertone of preparation and possibility.

- Guaranteed results (we never promise specific outcomes)
- Easy money (in franchisee recruitment materials)

- Nerd / geek (even affectionately; we are inclusive)
- Busy work (our curriculum is purposeful)
- Screen time (when describing our classes; use "hands-on coding" instead)

## Voice by Audience

### Speaking to Parents
**Tone:** Warm, reassuring, informative
**Goal:** Build trust, address concerns, demonstrate value

Parents need to feel that Skill Samurai is a safe, productive, and worthwhile investment in their child's future. Lead with outcomes and safety. Follow with convenience and community.

- Use "your child" rather than "students" to make it personal
- Acknowledge the challenge of finding quality extracurricular activities
- Reference specific skills their child will learn
- Mention instructor qualifications and child safety standards
- Keep sentences short and scannable

**Example:** "Your child will build a working robot in their first term -- and the problem-solving skills to tackle whatever comes next."

### Speaking to Franchisees
**Tone:** Professional, supportive, opportunity-focused
**Goal:** Attract the right candidates and retain engaged operators

Franchisees are business partners. Speak to them as equals investing in a shared mission. Be transparent about the work involved and enthusiastic about the support provided.

- Lead with the mission, follow with the business model
- Be specific about support structures and training
- Use AUD figures and realistic timelines
- Reference the community of fellow franchisees
- Avoid hype; attract operators, not speculators

**Example:** "You will have a dedicated launch coach, a proven curriculum, and a network of fellow franchisees -- because building a business is better when you are not doing it alone."

### Speaking to School Partners
**Tone:** Collaborative, professional, outcomes-oriented
**Goal:** Position Skill Samurai as a curriculum-aligned enrichment partner

Schools want partners who are reliable, credentialled, and aligned with educational standards. Emphasise curriculum rigour, WWCC compliance, and flexibility to fit school schedules.

- Reference alignment with national curriculum frameworks
- Emphasise logistical simplicity for the school
- Provide data on student engagement and outcomes
- Offer flexible program structures
- Position as a complement to classroom learning, not a replacement

**Example:** "Our workshops are designed to integrate with your existing STEM curriculum, delivered by WWCC-cleared instructors, with zero logistical burden on your staff."

### Speaking to Kids (Student-Facing)
**Tone:** Energetic, encouraging, fun
**Goal:** Make coding and robotics feel exciting and achievable

Kids should feel like they are joining something cool. Avoid talking about career readiness (that is for parents). Focus on the fun, the projects, and the community.

- Use active, energetic language
- Focus on what they will create, not what they will learn
- Reference projects, not lessons
- Celebrate effort and creativity, not just results
- Keep it age-appropriate across all four age groups

**Example:** "Build your own game. Program a robot. Create an app your friends will actually use. This is where you level up."

## Do / Don't Rules

### Do
- Use active voice ("Students build real projects" not "Real projects are built by students")
- Include specific outcomes and data points wherever possible
- Address the reader directly with "you" and "your"
- Keep sentences under 25 words on average
- Use short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max in marketing copy)
- Reference the brand name as "Skill Samurai" on first mention, then contextual references thereafter
- Use Australian English spelling (colour, organisation, centre)

### Don't
- Never use fear-based messaging ("Your child will fall behind")
- Never disparage competitors by name
- Never make unsubstantiated claims ("the best coding program in the world")
- Never use jargon without context (explain "API" or "algorithm" if writing for parents)
- Never use all caps for emphasis; use bold or italics instead
- Never promise specific financial returns to prospective franchisees
- Never refer to children as "customers" or "consumers"

## Formatting Standards

- Headlines: sentence case for blog posts and articles; title case for program names and landing pages
- Numbers: spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above; always use numerals for statistics and financial figures
- Lists: use bullet points for three or more items; use numbered lists only for sequential steps
- CTAs: action-oriented and specific ("Book a Free Trial" not "Learn More")
- Dates: DD/MM/YYYY for AU and EG; MM/DD/YYYY for CA

## Content Approval

All external-facing content must be reviewed against this guide before publication. Franchisees may create local content using approved templates but may not alter core brand messaging, logos, or colour schemes without written approval from the brand team.
